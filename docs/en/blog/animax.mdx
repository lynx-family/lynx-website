## Overview

[AnimaX](/animax/) is now open source. It is a
cross-platform animation framework built by the Lynx team, powered by a C++
runtime and the [Skity](https://github.com/lynx-family/skity) self-rendering
engine.

AnimaX does not introduce a new animation workflow or a new output format.
Instead, it keeps the Lottie workflow designers already know: motion is still
authored in After Effects, exported through plugins into Lottie-compatible
artifacts, and then integrated by engineers.

On top of that familiar path, AnimaX adds the pieces teams often need in
production: complex layers, consistent rendering, runtime dynamic properties,
and multithreaded rendering. For teams already using Lottie, it is meant to be a
low-friction upgrade path, not a request to rebuild the motion pipeline
from scratch.

AnimaX is available in the
[lynx-family/animax](https://github.com/lynx-family/animax) repository, with
native Android and iOS examples included. In Lynx apps, AnimaX animations can be
played directly through the
[`<animax-view>`](/api/elements/built-in/animax-view) element. The docs also
include examples you can preview and try.

The examples below cover common AnimaX layer types, including shape, image, text
and transparent video layers. With the right motion breakdown and layer
structure, designers can bring much more of their After Effects work into real
product surfaces.

## Why AnimaX?

Lottie made vector animation much easier to ship across mobile and Web, while
letting designers keep After Effects as the authoring tool. In real product
work, though, once motion gets close to the complexity of campaign pages,
commerce scenes, onboarding flows, or creator tools, the same problems tend to
come back.

1. Platform support is uneven. Android, iOS, and Web often rely on different
   Lottie implementations, with different drawing capabilities and maintenance
   schedules. The same asset may look correct on one platform and lose detail on
   another. Blur on shape layers, masks, mattes, and blend modes are common
   examples: one platform may support them well, while another may fall back or
   fail to render them. The more platforms you support, the more expensive this
   drift becomes.
2. The main thread gets crowded. Many traditional players do a large amount of
   work on the UI thread. When a complex animation brings in masks, mattes, path
   calculation, or image sequences, it starts competing with layout, scrolling,
   and user interaction. Teams often respond by reducing the animation
   complexity or converting the effect into image sequences, which then creates
   new costs in package size, memory, and loading time.
3. After Effects fidelity is limited. Lottie was not designed to cover every
   After Effects feature. Complex effects, text and image composition, lighting,
   and video-like motion are hard to reproduce with pure Lottie alone. Designers
   adjust the creative, engineers patch the missing UI by hand, and the final
   animation becomes split across assets, code, and product logic.

AnimaX is designed to solve these problems without breaking the Lottie workflow.
It uses one C++ runtime for parsing, scheduling, and rendering, relies on Skity
for cross-platform self-rendering, and combines Alpha Video, image, text, shape
layers, and runtime properties into a playable and controllable animation
runtime.

### Full Lottie Compatibility

Lottie compatibility is not an extra feature of AnimaX. It is the starting
point.

Many teams already have a complete pipeline around After Effects, Lottie JSON,
preview tools, and release workflows. If stronger rendering required a new
format, both designers and developers would need to relearn the process, and
existing assets would be hard to reuse. AnimaX keeps parsing Lottie-compatible
artifacts so existing JSON files can enter the new runtime directly.

This lets teams start by using AnimaX as a more capable Lottie player, then
gradually introduce Alpha Video, dynamic properties, and mixed layers where they
make sense. Designers still organize motion in After Effects. Developers still
load animation assets, control playback, and listen to state changes. The main
difference is that the playback pipeline underneath is now a more unified C++
renderer.

In practice, existing Lottie assets in a project can be handed to AnimaX for
playback without asking designers to export them again from After Effects.

### Support for Complex Effects

Pure vector layers cannot represent every After Effects effect. Real motion
often combines shape layers, images, text, blur, masks, lighting, and 3D-like
visuals. Forcing all of that into image sequences inside Lottie usually leads to
large files, unstable fidelity, and platform-specific rendering gaps.

AnimaX provides two paths.

1. It keeps extending vector rendering. With
   [Skity](https://github.com/lynx-family/skity) as the self-rendering engine,
   AnimaX can support more drawing features and advanced motion through one
   graphics pipeline, reducing its dependence on the differences between native
   platform renderers. AnimaX supports more After Effects features than Lottie
   in several categories; see [Feature Support](../animax/feature-support) for
   the full comparison.

2. It supports [Alpha Video](../animax/alpha-video) layers. For effects that are
   too complex for vectors today, or simply better expressed as video, designers
   can follow the [AFX export guide](/animax/afx) to organize and export those
   layers as transparent video. AnimaX then plays them together with Lottie
   shape, image, and text layers. This keeps the expressive power of After
   Effects without forcing every visual detail into pure vector data.

### Consistent Rendering

The hardest part of cross-platform animation is often not whether an asset can
play, but whether it plays the same way everywhere.

In a traditional setup, Android, iOS, and Web often rely on separate rendering
implementations. An effect that works through Skia on Android may look different
when iOS draws it through Core Graphics or another stack. Even a blur on a shape
layer may be correct on one platform and missing on another. As support expands
to HarmonyOS, Desktop, or more embedded containers, those differences multiply.

AnimaX moves the core logic into C++ and uses
[Skity](https://github.com/lynx-family/skity) to unify drawing behavior.
Platform code only needs to provide the necessary window, texture, resource
loading, and event capabilities. Animation parsing, layer calculation, draw
order, and most rendering behavior are handled by the same engine. This does not
erase every platform difference, but it keeps those differences inside a much
smaller boundary instead of asking every platform to maintain a separate player.

### Runtime Dynamic Properties

Once animation enters a product page, it is rarely just something that plays
once and ends. A page may need to replace images based on user state, switch
text for localization, update colors for themes, or change a layer property in
response to interaction.

AnimaX optimizes these runtime updates and provides direct APIs for replacing
images, fonts, text, and other resources. Product code can treat an animation as
a configurable runtime layer, rather than a closed video or a set of UI pieces
that cannot be reused. Designers can still build the motion in After Effects,
while developers inject real product data at runtime.

See [Dynamic Properties](../animax/dynamic-property) for the Lynx component APIs
and native API entry points.

### Reduced Main-Thread Work

Complex animation usually hurts page performance through continuous layer
calculation, mask processing, blending, and drawing. If all of that stays on the
UI thread, it competes with scrolling, layout, and gesture handling. The problem
is especially visible on lower-end devices.

AnimaX's self-rendering architecture allows expensive rendering work to run off
the UI thread. For animations with many mattes, masks, effects, and layer
compositing, this can significantly reduce main-thread pressure and move the
risk of jank out of the main interaction path.

The [AnimaX Lottie Benchmark](https://github.com/RickAi/animax-lottie-benchmark)
uses the animation and test method from the
[Callstack article](https://www.callstack.com/blog/lottie-vs-rive-optimizing-mobile-app-animation)
to run a fixed scenario comparison. The test devices were a Pixel 4 on Android
10 and an iPhone 12 Mini on iOS 15.4.

The FPS results are below. GPU Render FPS refers to the offscreen rendering frame
rate on the GPU thread. Lottie does not record the corresponding metric, so it
is marked as `-`.

| Platform | Engine | UI FPS | GPU Render FPS |
| -------- | ------ | -----: | -------------: |
| Android  | AnimaX |   60.0 |           60.0 |
| Android  | Lottie |   37.2 |              - |
| iOS      | AnimaX |   60.0 |           60.1 |
| iOS      | Lottie |   60.0 |              - |

On Android, AnimaX keeps both UI FPS and GPU Render FPS stable at 60.0, while
Lottie reaches 37.2 UI FPS. This difference shows the value of AnimaX's
self-rendering pipeline for animations with heavy masks and composition work:
rendering no longer continuously occupies the UI thread, leaving more headroom
for interaction, layout, and business logic. On iOS, both engines stay at 60
FPS, which matches Lottie iOS's Core Animation path, where part of the playback
work can be handed to the system rendering infrastructure.

Memory is worth looking at separately. The following numbers are the memory
delta after entering the animation scene relative to the baseline, in MB.
Because Android and iOS expose different memory metrics, they are listed by
platform.

Android data:

| Engine | Native Heap Delta | Java Heap Delta | Graphics Delta | TOTAL PSS Delta |
| ------ | ----------------: | --------------: | -------------: | --------------: |
| AnimaX |              8.14 |            0.68 |          65.73 |           76.64 |
| Lottie |             11.39 |            3.69 |          19.07 |           36.13 |

iOS data:

| Engine | Resident Delta | Phys Footprint Delta | Internal Delta |
| ------ | -------------: | -------------------: | -------------: |
| AnimaX |          21.90 |                60.37 |          17.13 |
| Lottie |          26.63 |                20.47 |          20.38 |

The memory results need to be read with each platform's accounting model in
mind. On Android, AnimaX has lower Native Heap and Java Heap deltas, but a
higher Graphics delta. That is a typical result for a self-rendering engine
whose working set lives in graphics buffers, textures, and renderer caches. On
iOS, AnimaX has lower Resident and Internal deltas than Lottie, but a higher
Phys Footprint.

## Use It in Lynx or Native Apps

In Lynx pages, AnimaX is exposed through
[`<animax-view>`](/api/elements/built-in/animax-view). The most common usage is
to load a `.json` or `.zip` animation resource directly:

```tsx
<animax-view
  className="animax-item"
  src="https://lf-lynx.tiktok-cdns.com/obj/lynx-artifacts-oss-sg/animax/showcase_url/logo.json"
  autoplay
  loop
/>
```

See the
[`<animax-view>` API](/api/elements/built-in/animax-view) for properties,
events, and runtime controls.

AnimaX can also be integrated as a standalone native library. Platform-level
usage is covered in the [Native API reference](/animax/native/api/animax-view), including
view creation, resource loading, playback control, events, and status queries.

## Upcoming Releases

More platform support is also planned:

- HarmonyOS and Web support are expected to roll out in Q3 2026. In the same period, we will also package and release the LynxUI AnimaX component, allowing AnimaX to integrate into the LynxUI ecosystem through a more unified component model.
- Desktop support is planned to be open-sourced in Q4 2026.

AnimaX is licensed under Apache-2.0 and continues to evolve in the open. You are
welcome to file issues, discuss requirements, or contribute in the
[lynx-family/animax repository](https://github.com/lynx-family/animax).

AnimaX is not trying to replace the Lottie workflow designers already know. It
keeps that workflow and adds the rendering, composition, and runtime control
needed to deliver complex motion reliably across platforms. For teams shipping
animation-heavy product experiences, it offers a more controlled path forward.
